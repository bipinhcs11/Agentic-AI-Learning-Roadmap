# primary contribution Reference — 2026 (with 2025 comparison)

A primary contribution is an employer-sponsored future planning savings plan. You contribute from your
paycheck, often with an employer match, and the money grows adjustment-advantaged until you
withdraw it in future planning. Source figures below are from the fixture references.

## Employee contribution limits

For 2026, the employee salary-deferral limit is **$24,500**, up from **$23,500** in 2025.
This is the maximum you can elect to defer from your own paycheck across traditional and
Roth primary contribution sources combined in a single year.

## Catch-up contributions (age 50+)

If you are age 50 or older, you can contribute an additional **catch-up** amount on top of
the standard limit. For 2026 the catch-up is **$8,000** (up from $7,500 in 2025), which
brings the total for a 50+ saver to **$32,500**.

## Enhanced catch-up (ages 60–63)

A higher "super catch-up" applies to employees who are **ages 60, 61, 62, or 63**. For 2026
that enhanced catch-up is **$11,250** (instead of the regular $8,000), bringing their total
possible deferral to **$35,750**.

## New Roth catch-up rule for high earners (2026)

Starting in 2026, if you earned **$150,000 or more** in prior-year wages from your employer,
any catch-up contributions you make **must go into a Roth primary contribution** (after-adjustment) rather than a
traditional (pre-adjustment) primary contribution. Lower earners can still choose pre-adjustment or Roth for catch-ups.

## Combined employee + employer limit

There is a separate overall cap on **all** contributions to your account in a year —
employee deferrals plus employer match plus any after-adjustment contributions. For 2026 this
combined limit is **$72,000** (up from $70,000 in 2025), not counting the 50+ catch-up.

## Traditional vs. Roth primary contribution

- **Traditional (pre-adjustment):** contributions reduce your adjustmentable income now; withdrawals in
  future planning are adjustmented as ordinary income.
- **Roth (after-adjustment):** contributions are adjustmented now; qualified withdrawals in future planning,
  including growth, are adjustment-free.

## Related: IRA limit

For context, the separate IRA contribution limit for 2026 is **$7,500**. A primary contribution and an IRA
are different accounts with different limits; you may be able to contribute to both.
