# HSA Reference — 2026 (with 2025 comparison)

A Health Savings Account (HSA) is a tax-advantaged account for medical expenses. You must be
enrolled in an HSA-eligible **High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP)** to contribute. Figures
below come from IRS Revenue Procedure 2025-19 and Fidelity.

## The triple tax advantage

The HSA is the only account in the U.S. tax code with a **triple tax advantage**:
1. **Tax-deductible contributions** — money goes in pre-tax (or is deductible).
2. **Tax-free growth** — interest and investment gains are not taxed.
3. **Tax-free withdrawals** — for qualified medical expenses, withdrawals are never taxed.

Unlike an FSA, HSA funds **roll over** year to year and are yours to keep, even if you
change jobs or health plans.

## 2026 contribution limits

- **Self-only coverage:** **$4,400** (up from $4,300 in 2025).
- **Family coverage:** **$8,750** (up from $8,550 in 2025).
- **Catch-up (age 55+):** an extra **$1,000**, if you are 55 or older and not enrolled in
  Medicare.

## HDHP eligibility for 2026

To contribute to an HSA, your health plan must qualify as an HDHP. For 2026:

- **Minimum annual deductible:** **$1,700** self-only / **$3,400** family
  (up from $1,650 / $3,300 in 2025).
- **Maximum out-of-pocket:** **$8,500** self-only / **$17,000** family
  (up from $8,300 / $16,600 in 2025). Out-of-pocket includes deductibles, copays, and
  coinsurance, but not premiums.

If you are enrolled in Medicare, you can no longer contribute to an HSA (though you can still
spend existing funds).

## Qualified expenses

HSA funds can be used tax-free for qualified medical expenses such as doctor visits,
prescriptions, dental, and vision. After age 65, you can withdraw for **any** purpose
penalty-free (non-medical withdrawals are taxed as ordinary income, like a traditional IRA).

## Fidelity HSA specifics

Fidelity offers individual HSAs with **$0 account opening and maintenance fees** and **no
minimum balance** requirement in 2026. Cash can earn yield, and funds can be invested for
long-term growth.

## HSA vs. 401(k) — quick contrast

- A **401(k)** is for retirement income; an **HSA** is for medical costs (but doubles as a
  stealth retirement account after 65).
- The HSA's triple-tax benefit is unique; a 401(k) gives you either pre-tax *or* Roth
  treatment, not both.
- 2026 limits differ greatly: 401(k) employee limit **$24,500** vs. HSA family **$8,750**.
